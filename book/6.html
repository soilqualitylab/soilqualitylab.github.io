  1. Paul's teaching about citizens of heaven (Philippians 3:20) while living on earth creates tension between eternal identity and temporal responsibility. How do you maintain heavenly perspective while fully engaging with earthly concerns, and what does it mean to be "in the world but not of it" in your service?

  2. Jesus' prayer "your kingdom come, your will be done on earth as it is in heaven" (Matthew 6:10) connects divine rule with present reality. How might this vision inform your approach to making divine love tangible in broken situations, and what does it mean to pray for heaven's reality while working for its manifestation?

  3. The parable of the rich fool (Luke 12:16-21) contrasts temporal accumulation with eternal investment. How do you evaluate your use of time, energy, and resources from eternal perspective, and what does it mean to be "rich toward God" in your relationships and service?

  4. Peter's reminder that "with the Lord a day is like a thousand years, and a thousand years are like a day" (2 Peter 3:8) addresses divine timing. How might this perspective inform your patience with slow change in yourself and others, and what does it mean to work urgently while trusting divine timing?

  5. Paul's conviction that "our light and momentary troubles are achieving for us an eternal glory" (2 Corinthians 4:17) reframes present suffering. How might this hope inform your response to others' difficulties, and what does it mean to help with eternal significance rather than merely temporal relief?

  6. The rich man and Lazarus parable (Luke 16:19-31) shows eternal consequences of temporal choices. How does awareness of eternal judgment inform your approach to social justice and care for the poor, and what does it mean to serve with both compassion and moral urgency?

  7. Jesus' teaching about storing treasures in heaven (Matthew 6:19-21) challenges material security. How do you practice this teaching in your relationships and service, and what does it mean to invest in people and purposes that have eternal value?

  8. The marriage supper of the Lamb (Revelation 19:6-9) represents ultimate community celebration. How might this vision inform your efforts to build community and resolve conflicts, and what does it mean to work toward the unity that will characterize eternal fellowship?

  9. The new heaven and new earth promise (Revelation 21:1-5) includes God dwelling with humanity and wiping away every tear. How does this hope inform your response to present injustice and suffering, and what does it mean to offer comfort that points toward ultimate resolution?

  10. The final judgment scene (Matthew 25:31-46) reveals service to the least as service to Christ. How might this startling identification transform your approach to those society overlooks or despises, and what does it mean to see eternal significance in seemingly insignificant acts of love?
